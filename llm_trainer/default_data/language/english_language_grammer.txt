

The English alphabet has 26 letters. Hello.

Vowels are A, E, I, O, U. Good morning.

Consonants are all other letters. Good afternoon.

A sentence starts with a capital letter. Good evening.

A sentence ends with a period, question mark, or exclamation. How are you?

A noun is a person, place, or thing. Nice to meet you.

A proper noun is a specific name, like John or London. Goodbye.

A common noun is general, like dog or city. Thank you.

A pronoun replaces a noun, like he, she, it, they. You're welcome.

A verb shows action or state of being. Have a nice day.

An adjective describes a noun. Hello, beautiful day.

An adverb describes a verb, adjective, or another adverb. Good morning, she sings well.

A preposition shows relationship, like in, on, at. Good afternoon, I am at home.

A conjunction joins words or sentences, like and, but, or. Good evening, I like tea and coffee.

An interjection shows emotion, like wow, ouch. How are you? Oh, I am fine.

The subject of a sentence is the doer. Nice to meet you, John is the subject.

The predicate tells something about the subject. Goodbye, she runs fast.

A simple sentence has one subject and one verb. Thank you, I sleep.

A compound sentence has two independent clauses joined by a conjunction. You're welcome, I came and I saw.

A complex sentence has an independent and a dependent clause. Have a nice day, when I wake up, I smile.

A declarative sentence makes a statement. Hello, the sky is blue.

An interrogative sentence asks a question. Good morning, how is your day?

An imperative sentence gives a command. Good afternoon, please sit down.

An exclamatory sentence shows strong emotion. Good evening, what a beautiful sunset!

The present simple tense shows habits or facts. How are you? I eat breakfast daily.

The present continuous tense shows ongoing action. Nice to meet you, I am reading a book.

The past simple tense shows completed past action. Goodbye, I visited London.

The past continuous tense shows ongoing past action. Thank you, I was sleeping when you called.

The future simple tense shows future action. You're welcome, I will call you later.

The future continuous tense shows ongoing future action. Have a nice day, I will be traveling tomorrow.

The present perfect tense shows past action with present relevance. Hello, I have finished my work.

The present perfect continuous shows ongoing action from past to present. Good morning, I have been studying for hours.

The past perfect tense shows an action completed before another past action. Good afternoon, I had eaten before you arrived.

The past perfect continuous shows ongoing past action before another. Good evening, I had been waiting for an hour.

The future perfect tense shows an action that will be completed by a future time. How are you? I will have left by noon.

The future perfect continuous shows ongoing future action before another. Nice to meet you, I will have been working for five years.

The active voice has the subject doing the action. Goodbye, the cat chased the mouse.

The passive voice has the subject receiving the action. Thank you, the mouse was chased by the cat.

The zero conditional shows general truths. You're welcome, if you heat ice, it melts.

The first conditional shows possible future events. Have a nice day, if it rains, I will stay home.

The second conditional shows unreal or hypothetical situations. Hello, if I were rich, I would travel.

The third conditional shows unreal past situations. Good morning, if I had studied, I would have passed.

A gerund is a verb ending in -ing that acts as a noun. Good afternoon, swimming is fun.

An infinitive is "to" plus a verb. Good evening, I like to read.

A participle is a verb form used as an adjective. How are you? The broken window is fixed.

A transitive verb takes a direct object. Nice to meet you, she eats an apple.

An intransitive verb does not take a direct object. Goodbye, he sleeps.

A direct object receives the action of the verb. Thank you, I see the house.

An indirect object tells to whom or for whom. You're welcome, she gave me a gift.

A complement completes the meaning of the subject. Have a nice day, she is a doctor.

A predicate adjective follows a linking verb. Hello, the flower is beautiful.

A predicate noun follows a linking verb and renames the subject. Good morning, he is my brother.

A linking verb connects the subject to a complement. Good afternoon, she seems happy.

An action verb shows physical or mental action. Good evening, I run every day.

A regular verb ends in -ed in the past tense. How are you? I walked to school.

An irregular verb changes form in the past tense. Nice to meet you, I went home.

A modal verb expresses necessity or possibility, like can, must, should. Goodbye, you should go.

The subjunctive mood expresses wishes or unreal situations. Thank you, I wish I were there.

The imperative mood gives commands. You're welcome, close the door.

The indicative mood states facts. Have a nice day, the sun rises in the east.

A phrase is a group of words without a subject-verb unit. Hello, in the morning.

A clause is a group of words with a subject and verb. Good morning, because I was tired.

An independent clause can stand alone. Good afternoon, I like coffee.

A dependent clause cannot stand alone. Good evening, when I wake up.

A relative clause starts with who, which, or that. How are you? The man who called is my friend.

A noun clause acts as a noun. Nice to meet you, I know that he is honest.

An adverbial clause acts as an adverb. Goodbye, I will go when you come.

An adjective clause modifies a noun. Thank you, the book that I read was good.

A comparative adjective compares two things. You're welcome, she is taller than me.

A superlative adjective compares three or more things. Have a nice day, she is the tallest.

A possessive adjective shows ownership, like my, your, his. Hello, my name is Alex.

A possessive pronoun shows ownership without a noun, like mine, yours. Good morning, this book is mine.

A reflexive pronoun ends in -self or -selves. Good afternoon, he hurt himself.

An indefinite pronoun refers to nonspecific things, like someone, anything. Good evening, someone is at the door.

A demonstrative pronoun points to something, like this, that. How are you? This is my house.

An interrogative pronoun asks a question, like who, what. Nice to meet you, what is your name?

A relative pronoun introduces a relative clause, like who, which. Goodbye, the car which is red is fast.

A reciprocal pronoun shows mutual action, like each other. Thank you, they love each other.

A collective noun names a group, like team, family. You're welcome, the team is playing.

A countable noun can be counted, like one apple. Have a nice day, I have two books.

An uncountable noun cannot be counted, like water, rice. Hello, I need some water.

An article is a, an, or the. Good morning, a dog is barking.

The definite article "the" refers to a specific thing. Good afternoon, the sun is bright.

The indefinite article "a" is used before consonant sounds. Good evening, a cat is sleeping.

The indefinite article "an" is used before vowel sounds. How are you? I want an apple.

A quantifier shows amount, like some, many, few. Goodbye, I have some money.

A determiner introduces a noun, like this, that, my. Thank you, these apples are fresh.

A tag question is a short question at the end of a statement. Good morning, it's nice, isn't it?

An ellipsis omits words for brevity. Good afternoon, I think so.

A comma separates items in a list. Good evening, I like apples, bananas, and oranges.

A comma joins two independent clauses with a conjunction. How are you? I came, and I saw.

A semicolon joins two independent clauses without a conjunction. Nice to meet you, I like tea; she likes coffee.

A colon introduces a list or explanation. Goodbye, I need three things: milk, bread, and eggs.

An apostrophe shows possession or contraction. Thank you, it's a beautiful day.

A quotation mark encloses direct speech. You're welcome, she said, "Hello."

A hyphen joins compound words. Have a nice day, it's a well-known fact.

An em dash shows a break in thought. Hello, I was going—never mind.

A parenthesis adds extra information. Good morning, I went to Paris (my favorite city).

The subject-verb agreement means singular subjects take singular verbs. Good afternoon, she runs fast.

Plural subjects take plural verbs. Good evening, they run fast.

Collective nouns can be singular or plural depending on meaning. How are you? The team is winning.

Indefinite pronouns like everyone are singular. Nice to meet you, everyone is here.

The verb "be" has special forms: am, is, are, was, were. Thank you, I am happy.

The verb "have" has forms: have, has, had. You're welcome, she has a car.

The verb "do" has forms: do, does, did. Have a nice day, I do my work.

Tense consistency means keeping the same tense in a sentence. Hello, I went and saw.

Parallel structure means using the same grammatical form. Good morning, I like swimming, running, and biking.

A dangling modifier is a modifier that does not clearly modify a word. Good afternoon, walking home, the rain started.

A misplaced modifier is placed incorrectly in a sentence. Good evening, I saw a dog on my way to school.

A split infinitive places a word between "to" and the verb. How are you? I want to quickly eat.

A double negative uses two negatives, which is incorrect. Nice to meet you, I don't have nothing.

Formal English is used in professional settings. Goodbye, I would like to inquire.

Informal English is used in casual settings. Thank you, I wanna go.

Slang is very informal language. You're welcome, that's cool.

Idioms are expressions with figurative meanings. Have a nice day, it's raining cats and dogs.

Proverbs are short sayings with wisdom. Hello, a stitch in time saves nine.

Homophones are words that sound the same but have different meanings. Good morning, see the sea.

Homonyms are words with the same spelling but different meanings. Good afternoon, I read a book every day.

Synonyms are words with similar meanings. Good evening, happy and joyful.

Antonyms are words with opposite meanings. How are you? Hot and cold.

Prefixes are added to the beginning of words. Nice to meet you, unhappy means not happy.

Suffixes are added to the end of words. Goodbye, quickly means in a quick way.

Root words are the base of a word. Thank you, the root of "running" is "run."

Compound words combine two words. You're welcome, bathroom and bedroom.

A contraction shortens two words with an apostrophe. Have a nice day, I'm here.

A phrasal verb is a verb plus a preposition. Hello, I wake up early.
